What is loan amortization?

Loan amortization is the process of paying off a debt through regular, scheduled payments over time. Each payment covers two things: interest (the cost of borrowing) and principal (the actual debt reduction). Early in the loan, most of your payment goes to interest. Over time, the balance shifts — more goes to principal.

This is why the amortization schedule matters: it shows you exactly how each payment is divided, month by month, so you can see your loan's true cost and find the best payoff strategy.

1
Extra payments hit principal directly — even small additions early on can save thousands in interest.
2
Shorter terms = lower total cost — a 3-year loan costs far less in interest than a 5-year loan at the same rate.
3
Rate matters most early — a 1% rate difference on a 5-year, $30K loan changes total interest by ~$800.
4
Check your payoff date — knowing the exact date makes it feel real and helps you stay motivated.

How to use the Loan Amortization Calculator
1
Enter your loan details
Input your loan amount (the amount borrowed, not the purchase price), annual interest rate, and loan term in months or years. These three numbers fully define your repayment schedule.
2
Review your monthly payment
The calculator shows your fixed monthly payment — the same amount due each month for the life of the loan. This is split between interest and principal in varying proportions over time.
3
Explore the amortization table
Scroll through the month-by-month table to see exactly how each payment is divided. Early payments are mostly interest; later payments are mostly principal. Use this to understand when you actually start building meaningful equity.
4
Add extra payments
Enter an optional extra monthly payment to see how it shifts your payoff date and reduces total interest. Even small extra amounts have a significant cumulative effect over a long loan term.
5
Compare total interest
Note the total interest paid over the full loan term. This is the true cost of borrowing — often significantly more than the original loan amount on longer-term loans like mortgages.
💡 Pro tip: When making extra payments, always specify to your lender that the additional amount should be applied to principal — not your next scheduled payment. This is what actually reduces the loan balance and saves interest.

Loan amortization FAQs
An amortization schedule is a complete table showing every payment on your loan, broken down into how much goes to interest and how much reduces your principal balance. It also shows your remaining balance after each payment, from the first month to the final payoff.
Each month, interest is calculated on your remaining balance. When the balance is highest (early in the loan), the interest charge is highest — leaving less of your fixed payment for principal. As the balance decreases, the interest portion shrinks and more goes to principal. This is why extra payments early in a loan save the most money.
Any extra payment applied to principal directly reduces the balance on which future interest is calculated. This shortens your loan term and reduces total interest paid. Even one extra payment per year can save years off a 30-year mortgage. Use this calculator to model different extra payment scenarios.
Most fixed-rate loans use standard amortization: mortgages, auto loans, personal loans, and student loans. Credit cards do not use amortization schedules because the balance and minimum payment change each month. Adjustable-rate mortgages follow an amortization schedule during the fixed-rate period.
Most modern personal loans and mortgages do not have prepayment penalties. However, some older loans or certain lender types may charge a fee for paying off early. Always check your loan agreement or call your servicer before making a large lump-sum payment.
