What Is APR in Finance? A Plain-English Explanation

APR is the single most useful number when comparing loans or credit cards. Here is what it means, how it is calculated, and how to use it correctly.

MS
Written by Marcus Sheldon
Personal finance writer with 8 years of experience covering debt management, mortgages, and credit.
Published June 13, 2026  ·  Last updated June 13, 2026

APR stands for Annual Percentage Rate. It is the yearly cost of borrowing money expressed as a single percentage figure that includes both the interest rate and most associated fees. Lenders are required by law to disclose APR so that borrowers can compare the true cost of different loan offers on an equal basis.

Understanding APR is essential for anyone taking out a mortgage, personal loan, car loan, or using a credit card. It is the number that tells you what borrowing actually costs — not just the headline interest rate.

APR vs interest rate: what is the difference?

The interest rate and the APR are related but not the same thing.

The interest rate is simply the cost of borrowing the principal loan amount, expressed as a percentage per year. It does not include fees.

The APR includes the interest rate plus most fees associated with the loan — origination fees, mortgage broker fees, closing costs, mortgage insurance premiums, and other mandatory charges — all rolled into a single annual percentage figure.

Loan A Loan B
Interest rate: 6.5%Interest rate: 6.75%
Origination fee: $3,000Origination fee: $500
APR: 6.89%APR: 6.81%

In this example, Loan A has a lower interest rate but a higher APR because of the larger origination fee. Loan B is actually cheaper in total cost despite the higher stated interest rate. Comparing APR instead of interest rate reveals the true winner.

APR is always equal to or higher than the interest rate. If a lender advertises an APR equal to their interest rate, it means they charge no fees.

How APR is calculated

For a simple loan, the APR calculation incorporates the interest rate and any upfront fees by spreading those fees across the loan term and expressing the combined cost as an annual rate.

The basic approach: take the total fees, add them to the total interest you will pay over the life of the loan, and calculate what single annual rate would produce that total cost on the original loan amount. The result is the APR.

In practice you do not need to calculate APR manually. Lenders are required to disclose it on all loan offers. For mortgages in the US, the APR appears on the Loan Estimate document you receive within three business days of application.

How APR works on credit cards

Credit card APR works differently from loan APR in one important way: it is charged on your average daily balance, not a fixed loan amount.

To find your monthly interest charge on a credit card:

  1. Divide the APR by 365 to get the daily periodic rate
  2. Multiply the daily rate by your average daily balance
  3. Multiply by the number of days in the billing cycle

Example: A credit card with 24% APR on a $5,000 balance:

  • Daily rate: 24% ÷ 365 = 0.0658% per day
  • Monthly interest (30 days): $5,000 × 0.000658 × 30 = $98.63

This means nearly $100 of your monthly payment goes purely to interest before a single dollar reduces your balance. At a minimum payment of $100, you are barely keeping up with interest and the balance barely moves.

Types of APR on credit cards

Credit cards often have multiple APRs for different types of transactions:

  • Purchase APR: The standard rate applied to everyday purchases. This is the rate most people focus on.
  • Cash advance APR: Almost always higher than purchase APR, often 25–30%. Cash advances also typically start accruing interest immediately with no grace period.
  • Balance transfer APR: The rate applied to balances moved from another card. May be a promotional 0% rate for a set period, after which it resets to the standard rate.
  • Penalty APR: A higher rate triggered by missed or late payments. Can be 29.99% or more and may apply indefinitely once triggered.
  • Introductory APR: A promotional rate (often 0%) offered for a limited period — typically 12–21 months — to attract new customers.

When APR is less useful as a comparison tool

APR is most accurate when comparing loans with the same term. When loan terms differ, APR can be misleading because fees are spread across different time periods.

For example: a $3,000 origination fee on a 30-year mortgage adds very little to the APR because the fee is spread across 360 months. The same $3,000 fee on a 5-year loan adds significantly more to the APR because it is spread across only 60 months. Comparing the APRs of a 30-year and a 5-year loan makes the 30-year loan look relatively cheaper in APR terms, even if the 5-year loan costs less in total interest.

For this reason, always compare APRs across loans with the same term, or use total interest cost as the comparison metric when terms differ. The interest rate comparator lets you enter two loans side by side with different rates and terms to see the total cost of each.

How to use APR when shopping for a loan

Get multiple Loan Estimates on the same day. For mortgages, applying to 3 or more lenders on the same day and comparing Loan Estimates gives you an accurate side-by-side comparison. The APR on each estimate accounts for that lender's fees as well as their rate.

Check what fees are included. Not all fees are required to be included in APR. Title insurance, appraisal fees, and some third-party fees may be excluded. Read the fee breakdown on the Loan Estimate to understand the full cost.

Consider how long you will hold the loan. If you plan to sell or refinance within 5 years, a lower interest rate with higher fees may cost more than a slightly higher rate with lower fees. In this case, comparing total interest paid over your expected hold period is more useful than comparing APR.

For credit cards, focus on purchase APR. The purchase APR is what determines how much carrying a balance costs you. If you pay your balance in full every month, APR does not matter at all — you pay no interest regardless of the rate.

Frequently asked questions

What is APR in simple terms?
APR stands for Annual Percentage Rate. It is the yearly cost of borrowing money expressed as a percentage, including both the interest rate and most fees. It gives you a single number to compare the true cost of different loans or credit cards.

Is APR charged monthly or annually?
APR is expressed as an annual rate but charged monthly. Divide the APR by 12 and multiply by your outstanding balance to find your monthly interest charge. A 24% APR on a $5,000 balance results in approximately $100 in monthly interest.

What is the difference between APR and interest rate?
The interest rate is the cost of borrowing the principal. APR includes the interest rate plus most fees, expressed as a single annual percentage. APR is always equal to or higher than the interest rate and is the more accurate measure of total borrowing cost.

What is a good APR for a personal loan?
Borrowers with excellent credit (750+) typically qualify for personal loan APRs of 6–12%. Average credit (650–700) typically results in APRs of 15–25%. APRs above 30% should be avoided if alternatives exist.

The bottom line

APR is the most useful single number when comparing loans because it combines the interest rate and fees into one figure. Always compare APRs across loan offers with the same term. For credit cards, a high APR only costs you money if you carry a balance. Improving your credit score before borrowing is the most reliable way to reduce the APR you are offered.
