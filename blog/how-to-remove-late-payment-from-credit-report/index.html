How to Remove a Late Payment From Your Credit Report

There are two ways to remove a late payment from your credit report. One works when the information is wrong. The other works when you ask the right way.

MS
Written by Marcus Sheldon
Personal finance writer with 8 years of experience covering debt management, mortgages, and credit. All content is reviewed for accuracy against standard financial formulas and lending guidelines.
Published June 9, 2025  ·  Last updated July 7, 2026

A late payment on your credit report is one of the most damaging marks on a credit file — a single 30-day late payment can drop a score by 60–110 points depending on your starting point. And unlike high utilisation, which you can fix immediately by paying down balances, a late payment sits on your report for up to 7 years. That said, there are legitimate ways to remove late payments before the 7-year window, and understanding the process clearly is the difference between taking effective action and wasting months on approaches that do not work.

Can a late payment actually be removed?

Yes — in two situations:

  • If the late payment is an error. If the creditor reported it incorrectly and you have evidence you paid on time, you can dispute it and have it removed. This is your strongest position and the most clear-cut path to removal.
  • If the creditor agrees to remove it as a goodwill gesture. If the late payment was genuine but you have an otherwise clean history, some creditors will remove it as a courtesy. This is called a goodwill deletion. It is not guaranteed, but it works often enough to be worth attempting.

What does not work: paying the late balance does not remove the late payment notation. Paying brings the account current and stops further damage, but the historical record of the late payment remains. Debt settlement companies that claim they can remove accurate negative information through "special methods" are misleading you — the FCRA requires credit bureaus to report accurate information, and no legitimate process forces removal of an accurate record.

Step 1: Verify whether the late payment is accurate

Pull your credit report from all three bureaus at AnnualCreditReport.com. For the late payment in question, check:

  • The date of the late payment — does it match your records?
  • The amount — is the balance reported correctly?
  • Whether it appears on one bureau or all three (errors sometimes appear on only one)
  • Whether the account is yours — occasional mixed files affect reports

If anything is inaccurate, you have grounds for a formal dispute. If the information is accurate, skip to the goodwill deletion process.

Step 2: Dispute an inaccurate late payment

File a dispute directly with the credit bureau(s) showing the error — Equifax, Experian, and/or TransUnion. All three offer online dispute portals, or you can dispute by mail for a stronger paper trail.

Your dispute letter should include: your full name and address, the account name and number, the specific error (e.g., "payment reported late on March 2023 — I have bank records showing payment was made on March 2, 2023, before the due date"), and copies of supporting documents (bank statements, payment confirmations, screenshots).

The bureau has 30 days to investigate and respond. If the investigation confirms the error, the bureau must correct or delete the item and notify the other bureaus. If the bureau sides with the creditor, you can escalate by disputing directly with the creditor and filing a complaint with the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB).

Step 3: Goodwill deletion for accurate late payments

If the late payment is accurate, your path to removal is a goodwill letter — a direct appeal to the creditor asking them to remove the negative mark as a courtesy. This works best when:

  • The late payment was isolated — you have a long history of on-time payments before and after
  • The lateness was due to a specific hardship (job loss, medical emergency, family crisis)
  • You have been a customer with the creditor for several years
  • The account is now current and in good standing

Address the goodwill letter to the creditor (not the credit bureau — the bureau only records what the creditor reports). Send it to the customer service address or the executive offices address for better results. Include your account number, the specific late payment date, a brief explanation of the circumstances, your payment history before and after the incident, and a clear request for a goodwill deletion.

Creditors are not obligated to honour goodwill requests. Capital One, for example, has a stated policy against goodwill deletions. Others, particularly smaller creditors and credit unions, are more flexible. If your first request is declined, try again — a different representative may give a different answer, and a letter sent to the executive office often gets more attention than one sent to general customer service.

Which creditors actually grant goodwill deletions?

Goodwill deletion is entirely discretionary — no creditor is legally required to grant one — but published policies and reported customer experiences differ enough by issuer that it is worth knowing what you are working with before you write the letter.

Two things matter more than which issuer you have: how isolated the late payment was, and how strong your payment history is before and after it. Even at issuers with a stated no-goodwill policy, a well-timed executive-office letter occasionally succeeds — it simply succeeds far less often than at Capital One or Discover.

Step 4: Negotiate pay-for-delete (on collections accounts)

If the late payment has progressed to a collections account, you have an additional option: negotiating pay-for-delete — an agreement where you pay the outstanding balance in exchange for the collections entry being removed from your report.

Get any pay-for-delete agreement in writing before making payment. Send the payment after you have the written agreement confirmed. Note that the original creditor's record of the late payment may remain even after the collections entry is deleted — collections are reported separately from the original account.

Not all collection agencies will agree to pay-for-delete, and the practice is technically discouraged by the credit bureaus (though not illegal). It is most effective with smaller, third-party collection agencies rather than large creditors managing their own collections.

Frequently asked questions

Does paying a late payment remove it from my credit report?
No. Paying brings the account current and stops the balance from growing, but it does not erase the historical record of the late payment. The notation that a payment was made 30, 60, or 90 days late remains on your report for 7 years from the date of the original delinquency. To remove it, you need either a successful dispute (if inaccurate) or a goodwill deletion from the creditor.

How much does a late payment affect my credit score?
A 30-day late payment can reduce a score by 60–110 points, depending on your starting score and the overall health of your file. Higher scores see more dramatic drops because there are fewer negative marks already present. The impact decreases over time — a late payment from 4 years ago hurts far less than one from 6 months ago, even though both remain on the report.

What if the credit bureau says the late payment is verified and accurate?
If you dispute an error and the bureau verifies it as accurate (meaning the creditor confirmed it), your next steps are: dispute directly with the creditor providing additional documentation, file a complaint with the CFPB, or consult a consumer protection attorney if the late payment is genuinely erroneous and is causing financial harm. You can also add a 100-word consumer statement to your credit file explaining the circumstances.

How do I dispute a late payment on my credit report?
File a dispute directly with Equifax, Experian, and/or TransUnion — whichever bureau is showing the error. All three offer free online dispute portals, or you can dispute by mail for a stronger paper trail. Include your account details, the specific error, and any supporting documents like bank statements or payment confirmations. The bureau has 30 days to investigate; if it confirms the error, the late payment must be corrected or removed.

Should I hire a credit repair company to remove a late payment?
Generally no. Credit repair companies cannot do anything you cannot do yourself — they dispute errors and send goodwill letters, which are processes you can execute for free. Many charge $50–$150/month for services that yield inconsistent results. If the late payment is an error, dispute it yourself. If it is accurate, write the goodwill letter yourself. The only time professional help makes sense is for complex situations involving multiple errors or potential FCRA violations.

What is a goodwill deletion?
A goodwill deletion is when a creditor voluntarily removes an accurate negative mark from your credit report as a courtesy. It is most likely to succeed when the late payment was isolated, you have a long history of on-time payments, and the lateness was due to a specific hardship.

How long does a late payment stay on my credit report?
Seven years from the date of the original delinquency. After that, it is automatically removed. The impact on your score diminishes significantly in the later years, even before it falls off entirely.

Which creditors are most likely to approve a goodwill deletion?
No creditor guarantees goodwill deletions, but track records vary. Chase and Bank of America state they generally do not honor goodwill requests, citing accurate-reporting obligations. Capital One and Discover have no stated policy against them and approve some requests case by case. Amex and Citi have no published policy either way, and customers report occasional success with both. See the comparison table below for a full breakdown.

How to write an effective goodwill letter

The goodwill letter is your most practical tool for removing an accurate late payment. Most people write them too formally or too emotionally — both approaches reduce effectiveness. Here is what actually works.

Keep it brief — one page maximum. Long letters get skimmed or ignored by customer service representatives who handle dozens per day. Lead with your account number and the specific late payment you are requesting removal of. Then make three points: your payment history before and after the incident (quantify it — "I have made 47 consecutive on-time payments since that date"), a brief factual explanation of why the payment was late (job disruption, medical issue, overlooked due to moving — one sentence), and a direct, polite request for a goodwill deletion as a one-time courtesy.

Do not apologise excessively or include lengthy personal details. Do not threaten legal action — you have no legal basis to demand removal of accurate information. Do not imply you will close the account if they do not comply — this reduces your leverage rather than increasing it.

Send the letter to the executive offices address for the creditor, not the general customer service address. Executive office staff have more authority to approve exceptions and tend to respond more personally than frontline representatives. Search for the creditor's executive correspondence address — it is usually on their website under the investor relations or corporate contact section.

If your first letter is declined, wait 30 days and try again. Phrase the second letter slightly differently, focus more on your overall relationship with the creditor, and consider addressing it to a named senior executive rather than a generic department. Multiple attempts over several months succeed more often than a single attempt that is abandoned after one refusal.

Goodwill letter template — copy and fill in the blanks

[Your name] [Your address] [Date] [Creditor name] [Executive offices address] Re: Account ending in [last 4 digits] — Request for goodwill adjustment To whom it may concern, I am writing to request a goodwill adjustment to remove a late payment reported on my account in [month/year]. I have been an account holder since [year] and have made [number] consecutive on-time payments before and after this incident. The late payment occurred because [one-sentence reason — job loss, medical emergency, moving, etc.]. The account is now current and in good standing. I understand I am responsible for making payments on time, and I am not disputing that this payment was late. I am simply asking, as a one-time courtesy, that you consider removing this mark from my credit report given my overall history with [Creditor name]. Thank you for considering this request. Sincerely, [Your name] [Account number]

Keep it to one page. Do not apologise excessively, threaten to close the account, or mention legal action — none of these improve your odds.

After the late payment is removed: protecting your score

Whether through a successful dispute, goodwill deletion, or simply waiting out the 7-year window, the most important thing once a late payment is removed is ensuring it does not happen again. One late payment that drops your score significantly is a setback. A pattern of late payments is a systemic problem that no dispute or goodwill letter can address.

Set up autopay for every recurring bill — at minimum for the minimum payment on every credit card. Autopay at the minimum ensures you are never 30 days late even in months when cash is tight. Pay the full balance separately when you can, but protect the minimum with automation.

Set calendar reminders 5 days before each bill's due date as a secondary check. If autopay fails for any reason — insufficient funds, expired card, banking error — you have a 5-day window to pay manually before the due date. Most card issuers have a grace period of 21–25 days from the statement closing date; knowing your exact due date for each account removes the ambiguity that causes accidental late payments.

The bottom line

Start with your credit report — confirm whether the late payment is accurate before deciding on a strategy. If it is an error, dispute it immediately with evidence; you have a strong legal right to have inaccurate information corrected. If it is accurate, write a goodwill letter to the original creditor explaining the circumstances and your track record. Do not pay a credit repair company for something you can do yourself. And regardless of whether removal succeeds, the most important thing you can do for your score right now is ensure every current payment is on time — recent payment history carries far more weight than a single old late mark.
