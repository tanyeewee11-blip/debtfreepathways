Backdoor Roth IRA: How It Works and the Pro-Rata Rule Trap

A legitimate workaround for high earners locked out of a Roth IRA directly — with one detail that trips up more people than anything else in this process.

MS
Written by Marcus Sheldon
Personal finance writer with 8 years of experience covering debt management, mortgages, and credit.
Published July 11, 2026

A Roth IRA has an income ceiling: once your earnings pass a certain point, you can't contribute directly, no matter how much you'd like to. The backdoor Roth IRA is the standard workaround — contribute to a traditional IRA (which has no income limit on contributing, only on deducting), then convert that balance to a Roth IRA, since conversions have no income limit at all. Done correctly, it gets high earners into a Roth IRA through a side door that's fully sanctioned by the tax code. Done without checking one specific detail first, it can trigger an unexpected tax bill that catches people completely off guard.

1. Why this exists: the Roth IRA income limit

For 2026, direct Roth IRA contributions phase out between $153,000 and $168,000 of modified adjusted gross income for single filers, and between $242,000 and $252,000 for married couples filing jointly. Above those ranges, you can't contribute to a Roth IRA directly at all. There's no such income limit on contributing to a traditional IRA, and no income limit on converting an existing traditional IRA to a Roth IRA — the backdoor strategy exploits that gap between the two rules entirely within the tax code's own boundaries.

2. The basic steps

  1. Open or use an existing traditional IRA and contribute up to the annual limit ($7,500 for 2026, or $8,600 if 50 or older). Don't claim a tax deduction for this contribution — you're not eligible for one at high income anyway, but it matters for tracking basis, covered below.
  2. Convert the traditional IRA to a Roth IRA. This can typically be done online through your brokerage, often within days of the contribution clearing.
  3. File Form 8606 with your tax return for the year of the contribution and the year of the conversion, to formally document that this was a nondeductible contribution being converted, not new taxable income.

3. The pro-rata rule: where this goes wrong

This is the single detail that trips up more people than everything else in this process combined. The IRS doesn't let you cherry-pick which dollars in a traditional IRA you're converting. When you convert any amount, it treats all your traditional IRA money — across every traditional, SEP, and SIMPLE IRA you own — as one combined pool, and taxes the conversion proportionally based on how much of that total pool is pre-tax versus already-taxed (your basis).

Here's why that matters: if you contribute $7,500 as a fresh, nondeductible contribution, but you also have $42,500 in an old rollover IRA from a previous 401(k) that was never taxed, your total traditional IRA balance is $50,000, of which only 15% ($7,500) is already-taxed basis. Convert that $7,500, and the IRS taxes 85% of it as ordinary income, not 0%, because it treats the conversion as coming proportionally from the entire pool, not specifically from your new contribution.

4. How to check if the pro-rata rule applies to you

Before doing a backdoor Roth, add up the year-end balance of every traditional, SEP, and SIMPLE IRA you own, across every brokerage. If that total is $0 (aside from the new contribution you're about to convert), you're clear — the whole conversion is tax-free. If you're carrying any pre-tax balance from an old employer plan rollover or years of deductible contributions, the pro-rata rule will apply, and you should calculate the taxable portion before converting, ideally with a tax professional if the numbers are meaningful.

5. A common way around the trap

If your current employer's 401(k) plan accepts incoming rollovers from IRAs — many do — you may be able to roll your old pre-tax traditional IRA balance into that 401(k) before doing your backdoor Roth conversion. This clears your traditional IRA balance down to just the new nondeductible contribution, sidestepping the pro-rata calculation entirely. Not every 401(k) plan accepts incoming IRA rollovers, so this is worth confirming with your plan administrator before assuming it's an option.

6. Timing and investment growth

There's no mandatory waiting period between the contribution and the conversion, but many people convert within days specifically to minimize investment growth in the traditional IRA before conversion — any growth that happens between the contribution and the conversion is taxable, since it wasn't part of your already-taxed basis. Contributing in cash and converting almost immediately keeps this taxable sliver close to zero.

Why this isn't something to guess your way through

The pro-rata calculation applies across every traditional IRA you own, including ones you might have forgotten about from an old job. Getting this wrong doesn't just cost you a bit of unexpected tax — it can also mean incorrectly filed forms that are a hassle to amend later. If your situation involves any existing pre-tax IRA balance, an old rollover, or a SEP IRA from self-employment income, this is a reasonable place to spend an hour with a tax professional before executing the conversion, rather than working it out from a blog post alone.
