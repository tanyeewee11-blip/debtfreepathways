What Is My Full Retirement Age? Lookup Table by Birth Year

💬

After years of fielding retirement planning questions, "What Is My Full Retirement Age? Lookup Table by Birth Year" is one I've learned to explain carefully, because getting it wrong is expensive and hard to undo.

A quick reference for your exact Social Security full retirement age, and what claiming earlier or later actually costs or pays you.

MS
Written by Marcus Sheldon
Personal finance writer with 8 years of experience covering debt management, mortgages, and credit.
Published July 11, 2026

Your full retirement age (FRA) is the age at which Social Security pays your full calculated benefit — no reduction for claiming early, no bonus for claiming late. It depends entirely on the year you were born. This page is a quick lookup, not a debate about whether the system should change; if you're interested in the policy argument over raising or restructuring the retirement age, see our separate piece on that debate. This one is just about figuring out your own number.

If you were born on January 1st of any year, Social Security calculates your benefit as if you were born in the previous year — a quirk worth knowing if your birthday falls exactly on that date.

What claiming early actually costs

You can start claiming as early as 62, but every month before your full retirement age reduces your benefit permanently — the reduction doesn't go away once you reach FRA, it applies for the rest of your life. For someone with a full retirement age of 67, claiming at the earliest possible age of 62 works out to roughly 70% of the full benefit, a permanent reduction of about 30%.

What waiting past FRA actually pays

Delaying past your full retirement age adds delayed retirement credits, worth roughly 8% per year, up until age 70. For someone with a full retirement age of 67, waiting the full three years to age 70 brings the benefit up to roughly 124% of the full retirement age amount. There's no additional credit for waiting past 70 — claiming later than that just means giving up months of payments for no additional benefit, so 70 is a hard ceiling worth knowing.

Quick comparison at a glance

Claiming age Approx. % of full benefit (FRA = 67)

This table tells you your number — not what to do with it

Knowing your FRA and the rough percentages above is step one. Deciding when to actually claim depends on your health, other income, whether you're still working, and your household's broader retirement picture — factors this lookup table can't account for. We cover that decision separately in when to actually claim Social Security, since it deserves more than a table.
