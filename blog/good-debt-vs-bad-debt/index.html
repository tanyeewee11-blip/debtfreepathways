Good Debt vs Bad Debt: What's the Difference?

Not all debt hurts you equally — understanding the difference changes how you prioritise payoff and future borrowing.

Personal finance advice often treats all debt as the enemy — something to eliminate as fast as possible. But that is an oversimplification. Some debt genuinely builds wealth or expands your earning potential over time. Other debt drains money with nothing to show for it. Knowing the difference helps you make smarter decisions about what to borrow, when to pay off aggressively, and when to let debt work in your favour.

What makes debt "good"?

Good debt generally has two characteristics: it carries a relatively low interest rate and it is used to acquire something that grows in value or generates income over time. The return you get from the asset or investment exceeds — or is likely to exceed — the cost of borrowing.

Good debt also tends to have structured repayment terms, predictable costs, and tax advantages in some cases. It is the kind of debt that, when managed responsibly, puts you in a better financial position than you would have been without it.

Examples of good debt

Mortgage debt

A home mortgage is the most widely cited example. You borrow to purchase an asset — real estate — that has historically appreciated in value over time. Meanwhile, you build equity with each payment, benefit from potential tax deductions on mortgage interest, and often pay less per month than comparable rent. The interest rate is typically lower than consumer debt, and the loan is secured, meaning lenders charge less for the risk.

That said, a mortgage only qualifies as "good" debt if you buy within your means, stay long enough to recoup buying costs, and do not treat your home as a guaranteed investment. Real estate can and does decline in value in certain markets and time periods.

Student loan debt

Education debt is the most debated category. In principle, borrowing to increase your earning potential — a degree that leads to a higher-paying career — is a rational investment. Federal student loans carry relatively low fixed rates and come with income-driven repayment options and loan forgiveness programs unavailable on private debt.

In practice, the calculus depends heavily on what you study, what the degree costs, and what you earn afterward. A $30,000 loan for a nursing degree with strong job placement is very different from a $120,000 loan for a field with few well-paying positions. The principle is sound — the execution requires honest assessment of return on investment.

Business debt

Borrowing to start or grow a business that generates profit is textbook good debt. If a $50,000 equipment loan allows your business to generate $20,000 in additional annual profit, you have created a return far exceeding the loan cost. Small business loans, lines of credit used for working capital, and equipment financing all fall in this category when the business model is sound.

Low-rate auto loans

A car depreciates, so it does not fit the classic "appreciating asset" definition. But if you need a vehicle to earn income and can secure financing at a low rate — say, 3–5% — this can be reasonable debt. The key variables are the rate (the lower the better), whether you genuinely need the vehicle, and whether you are buying within your means rather than stretching for a luxury you cannot afford.

What makes debt "bad"?

Bad debt typically has a high interest rate and is used to purchase things that depreciate rapidly or provide no lasting value. The cost of carrying the debt outpaces any benefit from what was purchased. Over time, bad debt transfers your wealth to lenders.

Bad debt also tends to be easy to accumulate and hard to notice until it has grown significantly. The purchases feel small individually — a dinner, a gadget, a night out — but the interest compounds quietly in the background.

Examples of bad debt

High-rate credit card balances

This is the most common and most damaging form of bad debt. Credit cards carrying balances at 20–30% APR are extraordinarily expensive. A $5,000 balance at 22% APR costs over $1,100 per year just in interest — money that builds nothing. The items purchased on the card — restaurants, clothing, electronics — have long since been consumed or depreciated. You are paying a premium for purchases you already made.

Payday loans

Payday loans are among the most predatory financial products available. They typically carry APRs of 300–400% when fees are annualised. A $300 loan with a $45 fee due in two weeks is a 391% APR. These loans are designed to be difficult to repay in full, trapping borrowers in a cycle of rollovers. Avoid them entirely — an emergency fund, even a small one, is a far better safety net.

Buy Now Pay Later (BNPL) misuse

BNPL services like Klarna and Afterpay can be interest-free if paid on schedule — making them neutral tools. But when used to buy things you cannot afford, or when fees and deferred interest kick in after the promotional period, they quickly become expensive. The bigger danger is normalising spending beyond your means across multiple platforms simultaneously, making your total obligations hard to track.

High-rate personal loans for discretionary spending

A personal loan at 25% APR taken out for a vacation, new furniture, or a non-essential gadget is bad debt. The purchase is consumed; the debt remains. The only scenario where a high-rate personal loan makes sense is using it to consolidate even higher-rate debt — paying off a 29% credit card with a 20% personal loan saves money, even if neither rate is attractive.

The grey zone: debt that could be either

Many debts fall between clearly good and clearly bad. Context matters enormously.

Car loans can be good (reliable transport to earn income, low rate) or bad (luxury vehicle you cannot afford, high rate).

Student loans can be good (high-ROI degree, manageable amount) or terrible (high cost, low-earning field, private loans at 10%+).

Home equity loans can be good (used to improve the home and increase its value) or bad (used to fund a vacation or pay off consumer debt temporarily without addressing the underlying spending habits).

The question to ask with any debt: Will what I am buying be worth more than what I will pay in total interest, or will it meaningfully improve my financial position? If the answer is no, it is likely bad debt regardless of the label.

How to prioritise payoff between good and bad debt

When you have both types and limited extra money, the general rule is straightforward: pay off bad debt first. High-rate consumer debt costs more and builds nothing — eliminating it is an immediate guaranteed return equal to the interest rate.

For good debt with low rates — particularly a mortgage below 5% or federal student loans — the calculus is less clear. If your investments are earning 8–10% and your mortgage rate is 3.5%, mathematically you come out ahead investing rather than prepaying the mortgage. But this involves market risk, while paying down the mortgage is guaranteed. Most people benefit from a balanced approach: eliminate all high-rate bad debt first, then split extra money between good debt payoff and investing based on your risk tolerance and goals.
