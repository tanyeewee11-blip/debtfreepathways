How to Pay Off Your Mortgage Early: Strategies That Actually Work

Paying off your mortgage early can save tens of thousands in interest. Here are the strategies that make the biggest difference, with real numbers.

MS
Written by Marcus Sheldon
Personal finance writer with 8 years of experience covering debt management, mortgages, and credit.
Published June 13, 2026  ·  Last updated June 13, 2026

A 30-year mortgage is designed to take 30 years. But the interest structure of a mortgage means the lender collects the majority of their profit in the early years. On a $300,000 mortgage at 7%, you will pay approximately $418,000 in total interest over 30 years — more than the original loan amount. Paying off your mortgage even a few years early eliminates a disproportionate amount of that interest.

This guide covers the most effective strategies to pay down your mortgage faster, the exact numbers behind each approach, and how to decide which combination works for your budget. Use the free mortgage calculator to model your specific situation.

Why paying off a mortgage early saves so much money

Mortgage interest is calculated on your outstanding balance each month. In the early years of a 30-year mortgage, the vast majority of each payment goes to interest rather than principal. On a $300,000 loan at 7%, your first monthly payment of $1,996 breaks down as approximately $1,750 in interest and only $246 toward principal.

This means any extra payment made early in the loan has an outsized impact. An extra $246 in month one doubles the amount going to principal that month, which reduces the balance on which next month's interest is calculated, which slightly increases how much of next month's regular payment goes to principal, and so on. The compounding effect works in your favour the earlier you start.

The result: strategies that seem small — an extra $200 per month, or one extra payment per year — produce savings of $40,000–$80,000 over the life of the loan and cut years off the term.

Strategy 1: Make biweekly payments

This is the simplest strategy with zero lifestyle impact. Instead of making one full mortgage payment per month, you pay half your monthly payment every two weeks.

The mathematics: there are 52 weeks in a year, which means 26 biweekly half-payments. That equals 13 full monthly payments instead of 12. You make one extra full payment per year without ever writing a large extra cheque.

Important: Contact your lender before switching to biweekly payments. Some lenders hold the half-payment until the full amount is received rather than applying it immediately, which eliminates the benefit. Confirm your lender applies partial payments as received, or simply make one extra full payment per year yourself.

Strategy 2: Add a fixed extra amount each month

Adding a consistent extra amount to every mortgage payment is the most straightforward way to pay down the principal faster. The key is to specify that the extra amount must be applied to principal only — not to future payments.

Even $100 extra per month saves $52,000 and eliminates 4 years of payments. The return on that $100 is extraordinary compared to almost any other use of that money at a 7% mortgage rate.

Strategy 3: Apply windfalls directly to principal

Tax refunds, work bonuses, inheritance, side income, or any unexpected money applied directly to mortgage principal produces immediate and compounding interest savings.

A $5,000 lump sum applied to principal in year 5 of a $300,000 mortgage at 7% saves approximately $18,000–$22,000 in total interest over the remaining term. That is a 3.6x to 4.4x return on the lump sum — guaranteed and risk-free, because it is a known interest rate.

The earlier in the loan term a lump sum is applied, the greater the impact. A $5,000 payment in year 1 saves more than a $5,000 payment in year 15 because it reduces the balance during the period when interest charges are highest.

Strategy 4: Refinance to a shorter term

Refinancing from a 30-year mortgage to a 15-year mortgage commits you to paying off the loan in half the time. The interest rate on a 15-year mortgage is also typically 0.5–0.75% lower than a 30-year mortgage, which compounds the savings.

$300,000 / 30yr / 7.0%$1,996$418,527
$300,000 / 15yr / 6.25%$2,572$162,960

The 15-year mortgage costs $576 more per month but saves $255,567 in total interest. The trade-off is a higher required monthly payment that reduces financial flexibility. If income drops, the higher payment becomes harder to maintain.

A middle-ground approach: keep the 30-year mortgage (lower required payment, more flexibility) but consistently make extra payments that match the 15-year payment schedule. This produces similar interest savings with the option to revert to the lower minimum payment if circumstances change.

Strategy 5: Round up your payment

This is the lowest-effort strategy and produces meaningful savings over time. If your monthly payment is $1,847, pay $1,900 or $2,000. The extra amount goes straight to principal.

Rounding up by $100–$150 per month on a 30-year mortgage typically reduces the term by 3–4 years and saves $40,000–$60,000 in interest. The psychological advantage is that the extra amount feels negligible compared to the base payment.

What to check before making extra payments

Prepayment penalties: Some mortgages — particularly older ones or certain adjustable-rate products — include prepayment penalty clauses that charge a fee for paying off the loan ahead of schedule. Check your loan documents before making large extra payments. Most standard mortgages originated in the last decade do not have prepayment penalties.

Principal designation: When making extra payments, explicitly instruct your lender or servicer to apply the additional amount to principal, not to future scheduled payments. Many servicers will apply extra payments to the next month's payment by default, which does not accelerate payoff.

Emergency fund first: Before making extra mortgage payments, ensure you have 3–6 months of essential expenses in an accessible savings account. Accelerating mortgage payoff at the cost of financial security creates a different risk. A paid-down mortgage does not help you if you cannot meet expenses during a job loss.

High-interest debt first: If you carry credit card or personal loan debt at rates above your mortgage rate, pay those off before accelerating the mortgage. A 20% credit card balance costs far more than a 7% mortgage. The mathematically correct order is: highest-rate debt first, then mortgage prepayment.

Frequently asked questions

How much do I save by paying off my mortgage 5 years early?
On a $300,000 30-year mortgage at 7%, paying it off 5 years early saves approximately $60,000–$80,000 in interest. The exact amount depends on when you start making extra payments and how much extra you add.

Is it better to pay extra on principal or make extra mortgage payments?
They are the same thing if done correctly. When making extra payments, specify that the additional amount should be applied to principal only. Some lenders automatically apply extra payments to future scheduled payments rather than reducing principal.

Does paying off a mortgage early hurt your credit score?
Paying off a mortgage may cause a temporary minor dip because it closes an account and reduces your credit mix. The financial benefit of eliminating mortgage interest far outweighs any short-term credit score impact.

What is the biweekly mortgage payment strategy?
Instead of one full payment per month, you pay half every two weeks. This results in 26 half-payments per year, equal to 13 full payments instead of 12. That one extra payment per year typically reduces a 30-year mortgage by 4–5 years.

The $50,000-$80,000 range depends on your loan size

That figure came from a specific loan balance and rate — the same strategies applied to a smaller mortgage or a lower rate will save meaningfully less in absolute dollars, even though the percentage savings can be similar. Run your own numbers through the calculator above; the strategies work the same way regardless of loan size, but the dollar payoff scales with your balance.

The bottom line

You do not need a dramatic strategy to pay off your mortgage early. The biweekly payment switch, rounding up your payment, or adding $100–$200 extra per month all produce savings in the $50,000–$80,000 range over a 30-year mortgage. Start with whatever amount is sustainable, specify it goes to principal, and let the compounding math do the work. The earlier you start, the larger the return on every extra dollar.
